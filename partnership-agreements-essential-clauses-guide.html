Partnership Agreements: What Every Business Partner Must Put in Writing

June, 2026 By Em nuel

Starting a business with a partner is like entering a marriage—without a prenup. The excitement of launching a venture often overshadows the critical need for a written agreement. But the reality is stark: partnerships fail not because of bad ideas, but because of preventable legal issues: ownership disputes, profit-sharing confusion, and unresolvable deadlocks .

In 2026, as 846,000 new companies launch annually, having a comprehensive partnership agreement is more essential than ever . This guide explains the 10 essential clauses every partnership agreement must include, with statutory references and practical examples to protect your business and your relationships.

The Default: If You Don't Write It, The Law Writes It For You

Without a partnership agreement, you are governed by default state partnership laws. In Massachusetts, for example, Chapter 108A § 18 establishes default rules that may not match your intentions .

Massachusetts General Laws, Chapter 108A, § 18

Equal Sharing of Profits

Each partner shares equally in profits, regardless of capital contribution

Equal Management Rights

All partners have equal rights in management, even with unequal ownership

No Salaries

No partner is entitled to remuneration for acting in the partnership business

Unanimous Consent for New Partners

No person can become a partner without consent of all existing partners

Critical Warning: Default rules apply automatically—and they may not reflect what you and your partner actually agreed to. A written agreement overrides these defaults and puts you in control.

Ownership & Equity

CLAUSE #1

Defines exactly how much of the business each partner owns. But equity can shift over time due to new investments, reduced involvement, or performance .

Example: Two founders launch a design firm. One invests $75,000 and works full-time; the other invests $25,000 and works part-time. The agreement can tie ownership to capital invested, time committed, or both .
"Ownership determines how profits are shared, how much influence a partner has in decision-making, and what happens in a sale or dissolution. Without clarity, you invite assumptions that can cause tension or disputes."
— Insogna CPA

CPA insight: Your tax accountant will ensure ownership percentages match capital accounts and flow correctly through tax returns, which matters for lender and investor confidence .

Profit Sharing & Compensation

CLAUSE #2

Partnerships can divide profits equally, based on revenue generated, hours worked, or ownership percentage. Also decide on salaries, guaranteed payments, and draws .

Example: In a professional services firm, two partners might agree on base salaries plus year-end distributions tied to client revenue each brings in .
"The wrong structure can create imbalance in workloads, incentives, and even tax liabilities."
— Insogna CPA

Tax modeling: A tax professional can model the after-tax effect of different profit-sharing arrangements to avoid surprises at filing time .

Capital Contributions

CLAUSE #3

Sets expectations for what each partner brings initially (cash, property, IP, services) and how future funding needs will be met .

Example: A restaurant partnership requires all partners to contribute proportionally to additional capital calls. If one cannot, their ownership percentage adjusts accordingly .

Must include:

  • Initial contributions (cash, property, IP, services)
  • Mandatory vs optional future contributions
  • Impact on ownership percentages
  • Partner loans vs capital contributions
Under Massachusetts law, partners are entitled to interest on advances beyond agreed capital .

Decision-Making & Voting

CLAUSE #4

Establishing a clear method for decision-making helps maintain operational flow and minimize potential conflicts .

Key considerations:

  • Consensus vs majority vote
  • Weighted voting by ownership stake
  • Matters requiring unanimous consent
  • Supermajority thresholds for critical decisions
"Equal ownership structures without established tie-breaking mechanisms create vulnerable decision-making frameworks."
— Attorney Aaron Hall

Massachusetts default rule: All partners have equal rights in management . Override this in your agreement.

Dispute Resolution & Deadlock

CLAUSE #5

Disagreements are inevitable. The question is not whether they will happen, but how you will resolve them without damaging the business .

Step 1: Negotiation

Mandatory direct discussion period

Step 2: Mediation

Neutral facilitator, non-binding

Step 3: Arbitration

Binding, enforceable decision

Step 4: Buy-Sell

Predetermined valuation, exit terms

Example clause: "Disputes require mediation within 30 days, with binding arbitration if mediation fails" .

Escalation protocols with clear timelines prevent disputes from dragging . Include cooling-off periods for major decisions .

Exit Terms & Dissociation

CLAUSE #6

Addresses how a partner can leave (voluntarily or involuntarily) and what happens to their ownership interest .

Statutory dissociation events (Vermont/Florida) :

Voluntary

Express will to withdraw

Expulsion

Per agreement or unanimous vote

Unlawful

Carrying on business becomes illegal

Judicial

Wrongful conduct materially affecting business

Bankruptcy

Debtor in bankruptcy

Death/Incapacity

Individual partner's death or guardian appointment

Example: A retail business with three partners. One retires. The exit clause states ownership valued at a multiple of average net profit, paid over 24 months .

Judicial expulsion standard: "Partner engaged in wrongful conduct that adversely and materially affected the partnership business" .

CLAUSE #7: Tax Allocation and K-1 Distribution Clarity

Partnerships pass income, deductions, and credits through to partners via Schedule K-1. But the income reported for tax purposes may not match cash distributions—a common source of confusion .

Example: A real estate partnership allocates depreciation equally among partners but makes distributions based on available cash after expenses. The agreement includes a clause for special tax distributions so partners aren't paying taxes out-of-pocket for income they didn't receive .

Must clarify:

  • How taxable income is allocated (can differ from cash distributions)
  • When and how K-1s will be issued
  • Whether tax distributions will be made to cover partner liabilities
A CPA can design allocation methods that comply with IRS rules while being fair to all partners .

CLAUSE #8: IP, Confidentiality, and Non-Compete

To protect the valuable assets of the company, partnership agreements must include robust IP and confidentiality provisions .

Sample IP Assignment Clause :

"Party A has exclusive and proprietary rights and interests in all rights, ownership, interests and intellectual property rights arising out of or created as a result of the performance of the Agreement, including but not limited to copyright, patent, patent application, trademark, software, know-how and trade secrets, whether developed by Party A or Party B."

Essential protections:

Confidentiality (NDA) Non-Compete (where enforceable) Non-Solicitation of clients Non-Solicitation of employees IP Assignment
Critical: Without a proper IP assignment clause, a partner who develops software or content may retain ownership, not the partnership .

These clauses survive termination of the agreement .

New Partners & Admission

CLAUSE #9

Your agreement should clearly outline the process for adding new partners while minimizing business disruption .

Key considerations:

  • Approval requirements (unanimous vs supermajority)
  • Capital contribution expectations
  • Vesting schedules
  • Impact on existing ownership percentages
  • Written instrument requirements
Default rule (Mass. § 18(g)): No person can become a partner without consent of all partners .

Buy-Sell Provisions

CLAUSE #10

Buy-sell clauses with predetermined valuation methods enable orderly exits when resolution proves unachievable .

Must specify:

  • Valuation methodology (multiple of earnings, appraisal, formula)
  • Payment terms (lump sum, installment)
  • Trigger events (death, disability, retirement, deadlock)
  • Right of first refusal
"Buy-sell provisions with predetermined valuation methods provide essential exit mechanisms when resolution proves unachievable."
— Attorney Aaron Hall

Governing Law and Jurisdiction

Every partnership agreement should specify which state's laws govern the partnership and where disputes will be resolved .

  • Governing law clause: "This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of [State].
  • Jurisdiction clause: "Parties consent to exclusive jurisdiction in [County, State]."
  • Venue selection: Consider convenience, neutrality, and enforceability .

Asymmetric jurisdiction agreements are valid in B2B contexts if clear and objective .

How These Clauses Work Together

One of the biggest mistakes new partnerships make is treating these clauses as isolated topics. In reality, they're deeply connected :

  • Profit-sharing ties into tax allocation
  • Capital contributions can change ownership percentages, which then affect decision-making power
  • Exit terms may impact future profit splits
  • Dispute resolution clauses must align with buy-sell provisions

Working with a certified professional accountant who understands how the financial, tax, and operational pieces fit together ensures your agreement is consistent, enforceable, and practical in the real world .

Your Partnership Agreement Drafting Timeline

1
Initial Discussions

Discuss ownership splits, roles, contributions, and vision. Identify potential areas of conflict early .

2
Engage Professionals

Consult with business attorney and CPA to align legal, tax, and compliance needs .

3
Draft Core Clauses

Ownership, profit sharing, capital, decision-making, and exit terms.

4
Add Protection Clauses

IP assignment, confidentiality, non-compete, dispute resolution, governing law.

5
Review and Sign

All partners should have independent legal review. Execute as a deed .

Common Pitfalls When Clauses Are Missing

  • No exit clause: A partner leaves unexpectedly, and remaining partners have no agreed method to value or buy out their share .
  • No tax allocation clarity: Partners get large K-1s showing income they never received, forcing them to cover taxes from personal funds .
  • No capital contribution rules: One partner consistently invests more cash, creating resentment and confusion about ownership .
  • No dispute resolution: Deadlock paralyzes the business, leading to dissolution or costly litigation .
  • No IP assignment: Partner who developed software leaves and claims ownership, destroying company value.

Put It in Writing

A partnership agreement is more than a legal formality—it is your operating blueprint, your mutual understanding in writing, and your go-to reference when questions or challenges arise .

  • Default rules apply automatically—Massachusetts § 18 mandates equal profits, equal management, and no salaries unless you override them in writing .
  • Ownership and profit sharing must be explicit, with mechanisms for future shifts .
  • Dispute resolution clauses with mediation and arbitration prevent costly deadlocks .
  • Dissociation events—death, bankruptcy, expulsion, judicial removal—must be defined .
  • IP and confidentiality clauses protect your most valuable assets .
  • Tax allocation clarity prevents surprises at filing time .

A well-structured partnership agreement aligned with legal and tax requirements ensures you and your partners can focus on building the business you envisioned .
