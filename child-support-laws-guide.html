Child Support Laws: How Payments Are Calculated and Enforced

June, 2026 By Em nuel

Child support is one of the most misunderstood areas of family law. For parents paying support, the amounts can feel arbitrary and overwhelming. For parents receiving support, the process of collecting what's owed can seem impossible. The reality is that child support is governed by a complex web of federal requirements and state-specific guidelines designed to ensure that children receive consistent financial support from both parents, regardless of their living arrangements .

In 2026, several states have updated their child support guidelines, including Washington's significant increase to the self-support reserve and Virginia's continued use of its detailed income shares schedule . This comprehensive guide explains exactly how child support is calculated, what factors courts consider, and the powerful enforcement tools available when payments aren't made.

The Three Models of Child Support Calculation

Income Shares Model

Used by most states. Calculates support based on what parents would spend if they lived together .

States: Most U.S. states, including Virginia, Texas, Florida

Both parents' incomes are combined, the total is applied to a schedule of basic child support obligations, and each parent pays their proportionate share .

Percentage of Income

Used in a few states. Support is a fixed percentage of the noncustodial parent's income .

States: Wisconsin, Connecticut, Alaska

Example: Wisconsin uses 17% for one child, 25% for two children, 29% for three children .

Melson Formula

A more complex model that ensures both parents retain enough income for basic needs .

States: Delaware, Hawaii, Montana

Includes a self-support reserve for each parent before calculating child support obligations .

Federal Requirements Every State Must Follow

45 CFR § 302.56 - Guidelines for Setting Child Support Orders

Based on earnings and income - Must consider all earnings and income of the noncustodial parent (and at state discretion, the custodial parent)
Self-support reserve - Must incorporate a low-income adjustment for parents with limited ability to pay
Medical support - Must address how parents provide for health care needs
Incarceration rule - Incarceration may not be treated as voluntary unemployment

Rebuttable presumption: The guideline amount is presumed correct. Courts may deviate with written findings explaining why the guideline would be unjust or inappropriate .

2026 Update: Washington Increases Self-Support Reserve

Effective January 1, 2026, Washington state increased its self-support reserve from 125% to 180% of the federal poverty guideline .

125%
Old standard (pre-2026)
180%
New standard (2026)

This change means that low-income parents retain more of their income for basic subsistence before child support is calculated. All of the parents' children are entitled to a pro rata share of income after application of either the self-support reserve, the 45% limitation, or both .

What If a Parent Voluntarily Underemploys?

Courts can impute income to a parent who is voluntarily unemployed or underemployed. This means the court calculates support based on what the parent could earn, not what they're actually earning .

Assets Residence Employment history Job skills Educational attainment Literacy Age Health Criminal record Local job market

Federal regulations require that when imputation is authorized, courts must consider the specific circumstances of the parent, including these factors .

Incarceration May Not Be Treated as Voluntary Unemployment

Federal law explicitly provides that "incarceration may not be treated as voluntary unemployment" in establishing or modifying support orders . This means parents cannot automatically have income imputed to them while incarcerated.

How Child Support Is Enforced

Wage Withholding

Income withholding is mandatory in most cases. Employers deduct support directly from paychecks .

42 U.S.C. § 666(a)(1)

Tax Refund Interception

Federal and state tax refunds can be intercepted to pay overdue support .

42 U.S.C. § 666(a)(3)

Liens on Property

Liens arise by operation of law against real and personal property for overdue support .

42 U.S.C. § 666(a)(4)

Passport Denial

Passports can be denied, revoked, or restricted for seriously delinquent support debts .

42 U.S.C. § 666

Financial Institution Levy

Assets held by banks, credit unions, and retirement funds can be seized .

Lottery & Settlement Interception

Lottery winnings, judgments, and insurance settlements can be intercepted .

License Suspension for Nonpayment

States have procedures to suspend various licenses when child support becomes delinquent .

Driver's License

Virginia law requires notice that a petition may be filed for suspension of any license upon a delinquency of 90 days or more or $5,000 or more .

Credit Reporting of Delinquent Support

States must have procedures to report overdue support to consumer reporting agencies. This can severely impact a parent's credit score and ability to obtain loans, housing, or employment .

42 U.S.C. § 666(a)(7)

When Does Child Support End?

In Virginia, child support generally continues until a child reaches age 18, but there are important exceptions :

High school students: Support continues for children over 18 who are (i) full-time high school students, (ii) not self-supporting, and (iii) living at home, until age 19 or graduation, whichever comes first .
Emancipated children: Support may continue for adult children with disabilities who cannot support themselves .
Arrearages continue: If arrearages exist at emancipation, payments continue until all arrearages are paid .

The Rule Against Retroactive Modification

Federal law requires that states have procedures providing that child support judgments are "not subject to retroactive modification." Once a payment is due, it cannot be retroactively reduced or forgiven .

However, modifications may be effective from the date that notice of a petition for modification has been given . In Virginia, administrative support orders may be modified from the date notice of review is served on the nonrequesting party .

Administrative Support Orders

State child support agencies can issue administrative support orders without going to court. In Virginia, the Commissioner may proceed against a noncustodial parent whose support debt has accrued based on subrogation to or assignment of a support obligation .

These orders become effective unless timely contested within 10 days of service . They have the full force and effect of a judgment and are entitled to full faith and credit in any other state .

Understanding Your Rights and Obligations

Child support is a complex area of law governed by federal requirements and state-specific guidelines . Key takeaways:

  • Calculation: Most states use the income shares model, combining both parents' incomes and applying state-specific schedules .
  • Self-support reserve: Low-income parents are entitled to retain enough income for basic needs before support is calculated. Washington's 2026 update to 180% of poverty level reflects this principle .
  • Enforcement: States have powerful tools—wage garnishment, tax intercepts, license suspension, passport denial, and liens .
  • Modification: Support orders can be modified prospectively but not retroactively .
  • Termination: Support generally ends at age 18, but high school students and disabled children may receive extended support .

The Child Support Enforcement program collected $25.8 billion for families in FY2024, demonstrating that these laws and enforcement mechanisms work when properly applied .
