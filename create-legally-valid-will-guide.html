How to Create a Legally Valid Will Without Mistakes

June, 2026 By Em nuel

Creating a legally valid will is one of the most important steps you can take to protect your loved ones and ensure your assets are distributed according to your wishes. Yet, even small mistakes can render a will invalid, causing confusion, family conflict, and costly legal battles .

60% of adults

do not have a will

40%

of those who do have significant errors

This guide provides a step-by-step roadmap to creating a legally valid will in 2026, highlighting common pitfalls and how to avoid them.

Step 1: Meet the Core Statutory Requirements

Testamentary Capacity

☐ ESSENTIAL

The person making the will (testator) must be of sound mind and understand:

  • What a will is and its effects
  • The extent of their property
  • Who would naturally expect to benefit
  • That they are making a will
The testator must not be suffering from any mental disorder that influences their decisions.

Proper Execution

☐ REQUIRED BY LAW

To be legally valid, a will must be:

  • In writing (typed or handwritten in ink)
  • Signed by the testator (or by someone in their presence and at their direction)
  • Signed in the presence of two witnesses who are both present at the same time
  • Signed by the witnesses in the testator's presence

Witness Requirements: Who Can and Cannot Witness

Must be competent

At least 18 years old and of sound mind

Cannot be a beneficiary

Witnesses should not be named as beneficiaries—they may lose their gift and invalidate the will in some jurisdictions

If a beneficiary witnesses the will, the gift may be void
Must be disinterested

Spouses of beneficiaries also should not witness

The Signing Ceremony: Do It Right

Step 1: Testator declares the document is their last will
Step 2: Testator signs in front of both witnesses
Step 3: Witnesses watch the testator sign
Step 4: Witnesses sign in the testator's presence and each other's presence
Step 5: All signatures on the same document
Key point: The witnesses must be present at the same time, and the testator must be present when the witnesses sign.

Step 2: Understanding Holographic (Handwritten) Wills

A holographic will is a will that is entirely handwritten and signed by the testator. While they are accepted in about half of U.S. states, they come with significant risks .

Not valid in many states; high risk of being contested

Holographic wills not valid

Holographic wills accepted if entirely handwritten, dated, and signed

Risks of holographic wills :

  • Harder to verify authenticity
  • May not meet formal requirements in your state
  • Prone to ambiguity and misinterpretation
  • Often challenged in court
Recommendation: Unless you are in an emergency situation (e.g., end of life), avoid holographic wills and use a properly witnessed, typed document.

Step 3: Name Your Executor and Guardians

Choose an Executor

☐ CRITICAL

The executor is the person responsible for carrying out the terms of your will, managing your estate, and distributing assets .

  • Choose someone you trust (spouse, adult child, close friend)
  • Name an alternate executor in case your first choice cannot serve
  • Consider professional executors for complex estates
Without a named executor, the court will appoint someone—often causing delays and additional costs.

Name Guardians for Minor Children

☐ ESSENTIAL FOR PARENTS

If you have minor children, your will is the only place you can name a guardian to care for them if you and your spouse die .

  • Name a guardian for the person (care and upbringing)
  • Name a guardian for the property (manage inheritance)
  • Consider naming successor guardians
If you don't name a guardian, the court will decide who raises your children—potentially against your wishes.

Step 4: Don't Forget Digital Assets

Digital Assets

☐ OFTEN OVERLOOKED

Digital assets like cryptocurrency, online accounts, and digital files are part of your estate .

  • List all online accounts, passwords, and access information
  • Provide clear instructions for digital assets
  • Store access information securely (not in the will itself)
  • Consider appointing a digital executor
Without access information, your heirs may never recover your digital assets. Studies estimate millions in cryptocurrency have been permanently lost due to missing passwords .

Where to Store Access Info

Do not include passwords in your will—it becomes a public document after probate .

  • Use a password manager with emergency access
  • Store in a secure location with clear instructions
  • Leave sealed letter with executor
  • Consider digital legacy tools offered by platforms

Step 5: Sign, Witness, and Store Your Will

Where to Sign Matters

Private Residence

Acceptable – ensure witnesses are present and not beneficiaries

Attorney's Office

Ideal – ensures proper execution and notarization options

Hospital or Care Facility

May raise capacity concerns – involve medical professionals to document capacity

Virtual Signing

Check state law – remote witnessing allowed in some states (temporary COVID rules may have expired)

Remote witnessing: Some states (e.g., California, Florida) have permanent laws allowing remote witnessing with video technology and specific procedures. Verify your state's current law.

Storing Your Will

Storage Options

  • Home safe: Convenient but risk of loss, theft, or destruction
  • Safe deposit box: Secure but may be sealed upon death, delaying access
  • Attorney's office: Secure and accessible; attorney often retains original
  • Probate court: Some courts allow filing for safekeeping
Tell your executor where the original will is stored.

Documents to Keep with Your Will

  • Letter of instruction (non-binding wishes)
  • List of assets and account numbers
  • Digital asset inventory (passwords – separate, sealed)
  • Funeral or burial instructions
  • Contact information for executor, attorney, accountant

10 Common Mistakes That Invalidate Wills

1

Failing to Sign Properly

Will must be signed by testator in front of witnesses. Electronic signatures may not be valid.

2

Insufficient Witnesses

Most states require two disinterested witnesses present at the same time.

3

Beneficiary as Witness

If a beneficiary witnesses the will, they may lose their gift and the will may be contested.

4

Using a Holographic Will Where Invalid

Handwritten wills not accepted in many states.

5

Lack of Testamentary Capacity

Will may be challenged if testator was not of sound mind.

6

Improper Revocation of Previous Will

Destroying a will improperly or failing to revoke earlier will can cause confusion.

7

Undue Influence

Will made under pressure or manipulation may be invalidated.

8

Failure to Account for Digital Assets

Digital assets may be lost forever without instructions.

9

Using Outdated or Generic Forms

State-specific requirements change; generic forms may miss key provisions.

10

Failing to Store the Will Safely

Lost or destroyed will cannot be probated.

Making Changes: Codicil vs. New Will

Life changes—marriage, divorce, births, deaths—may require updates to your will. You have two options :

Codicil: A formal amendment to your will that must be executed with the same formalities as the original will (witnessed, signed).

When a codicil is appropriate: Simple changes like updating executor, adding a small bequest, or changing a beneficiary.

New Will: Creating an entirely new will, which should include a clause revoking all previous wills.

When to create a new will: Major life events (marriage, divorce, birth of children), significant changes in assets, or multiple codicils making the will confusing.

Important: Marriage or divorce may automatically revoke parts of a will in some states. Always review your will after major life events .

After Death: What Happens to Your Will

The Probate Process

1. File with Probate Court
Original will filed with death certificate
2. Court Validates Will
Court confirms validity and appoints executor
3. Notice to Creditors
Creditors notified to file claims
4. Assets Distributed
Executor pays debts and distributes assets
5. Estate Closed
Final accounting filed, estate closed
The executor named in your will must be willing and able to serve. If not, the court appoints someone—potentially against your family's wishes .

Optional but Recommended: Self‑Proving Affidavit

A self‑proving affidavit is a sworn statement signed by the testator and witnesses in front of a notary public. It is attached to the will and can streamline probate by allowing the will to be admitted without the witnesses having to testify in court .

While not required in all states, a self‑proving affidavit can significantly speed up the probate process and reduce costs.

When to Consult an Estate Planning Attorney

While simple wills can sometimes be drafted without an attorney, you should seek professional guidance if :

  • You have a complex family situation (blended family, special needs dependents)
  • You own a business
  • You have significant assets or real estate in multiple states
  • You are concerned about estate taxes
  • You want to disinherit someone who might expect to inherit
  • You have digital assets or cryptocurrency
  • You have any doubt about your state's specific requirements

The cost of fixing a poorly drafted will after death is far higher than the cost of doing it right the first time.

Do It Right the First Time

Creating a legally valid will doesn't have to be complicated, but it does require attention to detail. A few minutes of careful execution can save your loved ones months of stress and thousands of dollars .

  • Core requirements: In writing, signed, two disinterested witnesses present at same time
  • Key decisions: Executor, guardians for children, specific bequests, residuary clause
  • Common pitfalls: Beneficiary witnesses, improper signing, failing to update after major life events
  • Special considerations: Digital assets, holographic wills, self‑proving affidavits

Your will is your voice when you're no longer here. By taking the time to create it properly, you ensure your final wishes are respected and your family is protected.
