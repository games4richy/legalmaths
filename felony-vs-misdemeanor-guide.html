Felony vs Misdemeanor: Understanding the Legal Consequences

June, 2026 By Em nuel

If you're facing criminal charges—or trying to understand what a charge really means—the difference between a misdemeanor and a felony matters more than most people realize. This classification determines how serious the offense is, where time may be served, how long penalties last, and how deeply a conviction can affect your future .

In 2026, multiple states are updating their criminal codes and expungement laws, making it more important than ever to understand the distinctions. From Virginia's six felony classes to Missouri's automatic expungement reforms taking effect this year, the legal landscape is shifting . This comprehensive guide explains the critical differences, state-by-state classifications, collateral consequences, and your rights.

1 Year

The dividing line between a misdemeanor and a felony

"Felonies are serious crimes that carry a maximum jail time penalty of more than one year. Misdemeanors are less serious crimes that carry a maximum jail time penalty of one year." — Garfield Heights Municipal Court

In Virginia, offenses punishable with confinement in a state correctional facility are felonies; all other offenses are misdemeanors .

Felony

The most serious class of criminal offenses — crimes that cause significant harm to victims or society .

⚖️ Prison sentence: More than 1 year up to life or death
Served in state or federal prison
  • Murder, manslaughter, kidnapping
  • Armed robbery, carjacking
  • Drug trafficking, large-scale fraud
  • Sexual battery, arson
  • Grand theft, embezzlement over $100,000

Misdemeanor

A less serious criminal offense — still resulting in a criminal record and potential jail time .

⚖️ Jail sentence: Up to 1 year maximum
Served in local or county jail
  • Petty theft, shoplifting ($1,000 or less)
  • Simple assault, battery without injury
  • First-time DUI, disorderly conduct
  • Possession of marijuana (under 20 grams in FL)
  • Trespassing, vandalism

Wobbler Offenses: When a Crime Can Be Either

Some offenses fall into a legal gray area known as "wobblers." These crimes can be charged as either a misdemeanor or a felony depending on the circumstances .

Weapon Involvement

Simple assault vs. assault with a deadly weapon

Extent of Injury

Minor harm vs. serious bodily injury

Prior Record

First offense vs. repeat offender

Financial Loss

Theft under $1,000 vs. over $1,000

Example: Second-degree murder (pre-2012) in NC is B2 felony; after 2012, same conduct is B1 with harsher penalties .

Enhanced Sentencing: Three Strikes Laws

In Florida, individuals with two or more previous violent felony convictions who receive a third violent felony conviction may be sentenced to a lengthier prison term under "three strikes" sentencing rules .

Repeat offender enhancements: A new charge that might otherwise carry a lighter sentence could be enhanced, resulting in a significantly longer prison term .

2026 Expungement Reforms: Second Chances

Missouri SB 1194

Effective August 28, 2026

Automatic expungement without petition for eligible offenses

  • Up to 3 felonies
  • Up to 5 misdemeanors
  • Any number of infractions
  • Court must notify when eligible
SB 1194 (identical to SB 531, SB 347, HB 352)

Maryland HB 0169

Effective October 1, 2026

Good cause expungement for misdemeanor or felony convictions

  • Petition after completion of sentence
  • Court may grant on showing of good cause
  • Denial not appealable
  • Must wait period before refiling
HB 0169, Pre-filed for 2026

North Carolina Automatic Expungement

For dismissals and acquittals on or after December 1, 2021, automatic expungement occurs 180–210 days after final disposition without petition .

Why Classification Matters

The difference between a misdemeanor and a felony is not just legal—it's social, economic, and potentially lifelong .

  • Misdemeanors: Up to 1 year in county jail, fines, probation—but still a criminal record that can affect employment and housing .
  • Felonies: More than 1 year in state prison, loss of civil rights, professional licensing bars, and immigration consequences .
  • Wobblers: Charging decisions depend on weapon use, injury, prior record, and loss amount .
  • Expungement in 2026: Missouri now offers automatic expungement for up to 3 felonies; Maryland allows good-cause petitions; NC automatically expunges dismissals .

Whether the charge you're facing is a misdemeanor or a felony, the legal system moves quickly. Without legal guidance, you can often miss critical opportunities to reduce charges or have cases dismissed entirely . If you've been charged with a crime, consult an experienced criminal defense attorney immediately.
